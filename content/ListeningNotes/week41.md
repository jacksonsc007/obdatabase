**英文原文：**

*   The business of nicknames
*   The Can of Ham cannot find a buyer.
*   It may be hard to see the Gherkin because the Walkie-Talkie and the Cheesegrater get in the way.
*   London's skyline is made of glass, steel and nicknames.
*   Sometimes these names start out as criticism: the city's tallest building got its name when it was described as a "shard of glass through the heart of historic London" by a heritage group.
*   But in time, they denote familiarity and, often, affection.
*   There are good reasons why buildings acquire monikers.
*   The Bottle Opener resonates more than the World Financial Centre Shanghai; the Lipstick Building is easier to remember than 885 Third Avenue.
*   Consumers give brands nicknames, too.
*   BMW owners in Britain drive "beamers"; in America they might beat the wheel of a "bimmer".
*   If you're a high-roller, you might wear a Rollie on your wrist.
*   You probably would not shop at Tarjay, an ironically Gallic pronunciation of Target, a mass-market retailer.
*   Brand nicknames are not always flattering: Neiman Marcus, another retailer, was once christened "Needless Markup".
*   But usually they suggest that consumers feel a genuine connection to a product.
*   In a recent paper Zhe Zhang of Western University in Canada and Vanessa Patrick of the University of Houston looked at how people react to the use of nicknames by other consumers.
*   In one experiment participants saw an online review of a new menu item at McDonald's; some saw a version in which the chain was referred to by that name and others saw one that called it "Mickey D's".
*   The chances that the review would be reported as fake were much lower when the nickname was used.
*   A nickname also made other people more likely to buy a product or pass on the review to a friend.

**第一句：“‘火腿罐头’（The Can of Ham）找不到买家。”**

*   **“火腿罐头” (The Can of Ham)**：这是指位于伦敦金丝雀码头（Canary Wharf）的**加拿大广场一号**（One Canada Square）。这座建筑顶部有一个金字塔形的尖顶，整体形状像一个巨大的金属罐头，因此被戏称为“The Can”。加上“Ham”（火腿）是为了押韵和增加趣味性，形成“The Can of Ham”的说法。
*   **“找不到买家”**：这并非字面意思说它真的卖不出去，而是指这个昵称**不流行、不被广泛接受或使用**。相比其他更形象、更朗朗上口的昵称（如“小黄瓜”、“对讲机”），人们很少用“火腿罐头”来称呼它，仿佛这个“昵称”本身“滞销”了，没人“买账”。

✅ **总结**：这句话是在调侃“火腿罐头”这个昵称不够成功，没有被大众采纳和喜爱。

**第二句：“由于‘对讲机’（Walkie-Talkie）和‘奶酪刨丝器’（Cheesegrater）挡住了视线，你可能很难看到‘小黄瓜’（Gherkin）大楼。”**

*   **“对讲机” (Walkie-Talkie)**：指的是**20 Fenchurch Street** 大楼，因其顶部宽大、底部收窄的形状酷似老式对讲机而得名。
*   **“奶酪刨丝器” (Cheesegrater)**：指的是**The Leadenhall Building**，其外立面布满垂直线条，从远处看就像一块奶酪刨丝器。
*   **“小黄瓜” (Gherkin)**：指的是**30 St Mary Axe**，即著名的“小黄瓜”大楼，因其圆润光滑、类似腌黄瓜的外形而得名。

*   **“挡住了视线”**：这是一个**夸张的拟人化表达**。实际上，这三座建筑虽然都在伦敦金融城（City of London）附近，但地理位置并不完全重叠，不会真的互相遮挡。作者在这里是用一种**俏皮的都市传说式语言**，把建筑比作有“身体”的角色——“对讲机”和“奶酪刨丝器”这两个“高个子”站在一起，就把“小黄瓜”这个“矮个子”给“挡住”了，让人不容易注意到它。
